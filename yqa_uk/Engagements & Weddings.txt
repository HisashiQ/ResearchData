<answer>



Unless this is a tiny wedding I don't think you have given yourselves enough time. Half an hour for the ceremony and the photographs isn't nearly enough, you are more likely to start the reception at 5.00. Then everyone has to greet you, the courses will be served, and then the speeches start. If that happens without a hitch and no one makes a long speech, best case scenario is an hour and a half but it's more likely to be closer to two hours. Then you start the dances, that leaves you cutting the cake at 7.30.
This all feels terribly rushed, as though you are trying to stuff all of the traditions into too short a time line. Why have dances when the guests won't be able to dance themselves?! I think they will feel cheated!



</answer>

<answer>



<evaluation>Best Answer - Chosen by Asker</evaluation>



Start as soon as possible, which others have already said. I'm getting married September 2009, and I've already tried on tons of dresses, if that gives you perspective.



Your first few visits will be trying to decide what style you want. Try on every style, even if you think you hate it. I tried on a few that I was dead against, but when they were on, they looked completely different, and I even liked a few of them!



After three stores or so, you can start to only try on styles you know you what. I'm now limiting myself to strapless gowns with dropped waists because I know that's what I look best in, and what style I want.

Order your dress at least 6 months in advance if possible. When you order a dress from the designer, they make it for you, so that's why it takes so long (it can take three to four months just to get it in). Sometimes, though, because of this, you can make special requests like, "remove the buttons" or "add a flower here." Emphasis on sometimes, not always. Depends on the store and the designer.

Fittings are included in the alterations price, not the dress price (two separate things - almost every dress has to be altered). Once your dress arrived, the store will call you and ask you to come in for your first fitting (you'll need your shoes and wedding day undergarments for that). They will tailor the dress specifically for you, add a bustle, and make any changes you want. Alterations are pricey. Some stores charge a flat fee, and others base in on what needs to be done. Usually, you can expect to pay anywhere between $200 to $500 for alterations.

You'll also have at least two fittings before it's perfect on your body.

Hope that helps! And really milk the experience for all it's worth. When I first started, I was totally intimidated by wedding dress shops and didn't want to do it. Now I love it and do it whenever I can :)

Good luck!



</answer>

<evaluation>Best Answer - Chosen by Asker</evaluation>



A lot of the wedding hubbub is commercial. Americans (mostly) put a lot of stock in weddings as a "status" symbol (Think "Platinum Weddings"!). Some people are born inot cultures where big events and formal parties are common and they don't see anything odd about investing so much money in one day. As for those who have a big lavish wedding and don't focus on their guests' enjoyment, I couldn't tell you. A sense of entitlement, I'd assume, but where that stems from, I am at a loss. I suppose the theory that "it's YOUR day" has a lot to do with it, and I assume that comes from society's view of weddings. I feel that the wedding is about the committment and the covenant you're making with your new husband and God. It's not entirely about you. It's about the 175 people who came from all over the place to see you make that covenant - so why not show them your thanks for coming and sharing the blessing? Sadly not everyone has a down-to-earth view on their reception or wedding - they want a glam, lavish, out-of-this-world party no matter what the cost or consequences.

More of the reception is based on what people can afford - and what they can't. No one wants to NOT be able to afford something (again, status quo, "keeping up with the Joneses") so they often go overboard on what people SEE (the reception) and then suffer the consequences of what people DON'T see (the credit card bills and debt). That's human nature, to present a face of "power" (often associated with money in the modern world) despite internal turmoil or fear. "Put on a brave face," they say in war, "and your enemy will be afraid".



My thoughts on the reception are "spoil the guests!". My guests come before anything in my thoughts and I want to surround them with a luxurious, pampering atmosphere. Budgetwise, we are lucky enough to be able to afford a luxurious wedding without breaking the bank, going into debt or taking money from other ventures. With our budget we are easily able to spoil our guests with extra servers, a 5-course sit-down meal, free transportation, open bar, tons of food choices, a great DJ, an awesome venue, extravagant favors and an atmosphere of elegance. I am always thinking of other things to do for them to make them feel pampered. I want my guests to leave feeling like it was THEIR day, that THEY were being honored by my husband and I and thanked for attending our ceremony. Had we not had the budget to ensure our guests are treated well, we would have ghad to find a way to ensure they were. If money were an issue, we would have a church wedding with a cake and punch reception in the fellowship hall. As long as my guests are able to witness and share the covenant, the reception is not an issue, it's simply a "thank you for sharing the moment". It can be as grand or simple as you want and can afford so long as the focus is on the covenant and the fact that your guests came to witness because they love you.



Wow that was long. This was a great question!!!
