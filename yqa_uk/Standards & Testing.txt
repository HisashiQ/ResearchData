<answer>



Well, I understand how frustrating studying can be, I'm now a 2nd year undergraduate doing BSc Mathematics and Comp Sci and it took me a lot of work to get this far.



These are the things I did to help me revise:



1: Get Organised



Lots of time is wasted when you cant find the one page of questions/answers to practise on so get some pocket folders with some dividers for each subject.



As for day plans you gotta be realistic about this, plan an hour or 2 a day doing some practise questions in your subjects. A good time to do so is during your lunchtime, an hour after school with the teacher (if he/she wants to) or perhaps in a local library. Key idea is to find a quiet spot with decent amount of space for your books and notes.



Worried to be made fun out of by your friends? Well to be honest they should respect you because put it this way they are not gonna get you the A grade in exams YOU ARE! As part of revenge watch them quibble in exam halls as you glide through the papers like I did on my A level maths paper with 74/75 *did a silly mistake on the one question lol* :D



And you dont wanna just revise, revise revise 24/7 every now and again you can have some fun like few hours playing a game or join your m8s to do things but dont make them take over too much of your time its all about moderation a happy + disciplined mind is a focused mind too.



2. Get the Revision Material



OK, now you got the basic idea how to sort yourself out, next stage is to grab the materials. Ask your teacher for a recommended book, grab past papers off him/her try and get at least 5 years worth of exam papers it may seem like a lot of work but once you did the first 2 years worth the next 3 should be a breeze especially in GCSE. The final tools then is a cheap notepad, a revise textbook (Letts brand) are normally good for GCSE/A Levels and my technique, postcards.



Why postcards? These are perfect to serve as your reminders of formulas and basic definitions on subjects like Maths and Sciences. Make a well organised plot of formulas that are likely to be on the exam and take the card in your pocket and every now and again look at the card when you're board such as a bus journey, a dull classroom or a TV advert to keep refreshing your mind.



OK, and finally begin the revision, I'm glad to hear that you have decided to begin revising months before exam. Knowing by fact that you will have a head start than your classm8s will give you plenty of time to have one-to-one questions with your teacher until the last 2 weeks comes up where every1 panic revise.



Firstly you read through the text book and note anything useful (not every single word and picture) onto the notepad and then finalise which formulas you should study and jot onto postcard.



Then, with basic theory in your head, practise on your first past paper. If you are stuck on a question look at the answer on the back try to understand why that's the answer and write it down and move on (by writing the answer on the paper you may notice by reading it gives you a message in your brain what to write down which should be a hint to remember). Obviously you must try to work out without looking up the answer, if there is an answer you do not understand, an error or whatever speak to your teacher to verify.



Once you did the first paper (eg Paper 1/5) do paper 2 then 3,4,5 then 1 again and continue. As you progress you should no longer need tolook upp the answers because the papers begin to repeat questions but with different values.



Try and avoid revising at home, use the school or local libraries where you are very likely to concentrate. Last thing you want is a relative/friend to keep bothering you every 5 mins.



OKthat's enough typing now hope this guide gets you somewhere, just stick to the plan and you'll do great.



</answer>

<answer>



I think a lot of the advice on this page is really useful... so its difficult to add more.



I just wanted to add, that not all revision techniques necessarily suit you: different people learn best in different ways. If you want more ideas and more specific advice, you could try asking teachers what they think is best for you (sorry if you have already). They might know you and see more clearly how you seem to work, so they could help a lot.



Also, there are many websites that can help offer suggestions. An entertaining example is  http://www.cgpbooks.co.uk/online_rev/exm... (really amusing!), but you can search for more techniques just generally on the internet.



Good luck with the revision!



p.s. reading the lengthy (:p) answer above, I think there is some really useful information there, but something to remember is to tailor the revision to you. Be realistic. Don't - whatever you do - think that planning to work for 5 hours straight is going to help. If you think that you're not great at concentrating (which, let's face it, many people aren't), then try to have regular breaks (without getting distracted and having a 5 hour break!!).



I really hope you find something that suits you!

You'll probably get a lot of questions that say the same thing, but here goes:

If you have a greater aptitude in science or math, take the ACT. People argue that it's easier because it takes less time and seems less stressful to test takers.



Take the SAT if you are strong in English. This is pretty much the traditional test to take, and the format is pretty easy to understand.

However, if the only thing you're interested in is which test is 'easier', and you don't wish to hear me rant on about how yes, it does depend on the person and it doesn't matter which test is easier, I can bypass all that and give the Reader's Digest version: The SAT is the easier test.

I do have to toss in here, at the bottom of the screen, that if you are asking for reference of your own standarized test taking endeavors, that the really smart thing to do is take both. Seriously. Although most colleges don't even require SAT or ACT scores anymore, they encourage them and they do help you get into a good college. If you take both, you can show institutions that you have a wide range of academic talents and interests, and are serious about impressing them and about furthering your education. Good luck!
